Introductory Comments (click to show/hide this section)

As you read through the interpretation of your scores, there are a few important points to keep in mind...

First, we discourage "typological" interpretations of scores. This means that we don't believe people should be organized into a limited number of personality types. Each of the personality characteristics described below should be considered a spectrum or dimension that spans a range of behaviors which are typically related. Some of these dimensions tend to be expressed in patterns that are more or less common across groups or cultures (as we point out in several places), but there is little evidence to suggest that any individual's scores on the various dimensions will match these cultural patterns. The number of ways in which these dimensions can be expressed is practically infinite and there is very little reason to categorize people according to a limited number of profiles.

Despite our views on typologies, it is common for people to wonder about the ways that personality dimensions relate to one another. The answer to this question varies considerably across groups of all sorts. Just a few of the factors affecting these relationships include age, gender, race, culture, education, and socio-economic status. If you are interested in seeing the way that these aspects correlate with one another in a recent sample of SAPA participants, click the link below. Keep in mind that this sample is not necessarily representative of any particular population.

On the Origin and Organization of the Aspects: All of the items used to derive your scores are taken from public-domain measures of personality. These specific items were identified because they are commonly used for assessments based on the five and six factor models of personality (especially the "Big Five" and the "HEXACO" models, respectively). The division of each factor into two aspects is based mainly on DeYoung's "Big Five Aspect Scales," with some revisions for naming conventions and the inclusion of two additional aspects (Honesty and Humility). The organization of the aspects -- and of personality in general -- remains an active area of research. At the highest level, the 12 aspects can be organized in three broad groups: Plasticity (the aspects in green), Stability (red), and Social Cohesion (blue). It should be noted that this highest-level of organization remains speculative. There is much more support for organization of the 12 aspects into five or six dimensional factors. They are Agreeableness (Compassion and Politeness), Extraversion (Assertiveness and Enthusiasm), Openness to New Experiences (Openness and Intellect), Conscientiousness (Industriousness and Orderliness), Emotional Stability (Balance and Boldness), and Integrity (Honesty and Humility). These are described in more detail below.

The scores for each aspect are generated using the stanine scaling method (see here for more info). This method produces scores from 1 to 9 (integer scores only), where the mean and standard deviation for each age and gender group is 5 and 2, respectively. When considering the meaning of your scores, keep in mind that the labels for these personality dimensions are merely used to generally describe differences between individuals. Your scores are based only on your responses in relation to others of your age and gender who have answered the same items. If you feel your results are inaccurate, we recommend that you take more items as this occurs most frequently when participants have responded to an inadequate number of items.

It's also important to recognize that personality is generally viewed as stable but not static. This means that personality often changes in accordance with other changes in life circumstances. While substantial, rapid changes to one's personality are unlikely, research suggests that learning, development, and therapy are just a few of the factors that can contribute to personality change.

Stability = Conscientiousness + Emotional Stability

Conscientiousness

By definition, a conscientious individual is guided by an inner sense of what is "right" (this inner sense being known as one's "conscience"). While they may hold dramatically different beliefs about what is "right", conscientious individuals typically share reputations for being meticulous, thorough and deliberate. The two aspects of Conscientiousness are Industriousness and Orderliness. Both of these concepts share a strong element of self-discipline, a feature which likely contributes to the similarity between Conscientiousness and traditional conceptions of "character." While the aspects are highly related to each other, their relationships with other aspects are distinct. Orderliness is negatively correlated with both aspects of Emotional Stability as well as Politeness. Industriousness is positively correlated with the Balance aspect of Emotional Stability and with both aspects of Integrity, especially Honesty.

Orderliness

Orderliness is generally valued as an aspect of personality because it is suggestive of tendencies towards organization, predictability, and cleanliness. Like all aspects however, it is not true that more orderly behavior is always better. An excessive desire for order can lead to perfectionistic behaviors and unrealistic expectations. In extreme cases, the inability to tolerate even small amounts of disorder may be associated with mental health issues. Orderliness scores are negatively associated with both the Balance and Boldness aspects of Emotional Stability as well as Politeness, and this may reflect the fact that highly orderly individuals are easily aggravated when others disturb their efforts to keep things "just right."

Industriousness

Industriousness is characterized by the virtues of diligence and perseverance and industrious individuals tend to be hard-working, efficient, and reliable. When given a task, they typically get to work right away and make a point of completing the task promptly and successfully. Given their strong work ethic, industrious people tend to be pre-occupied, at times, with tasks that less-conscientious individuals do not value. Industriousness scores are, on average, positively related to the Honesty aspect of Integrity, providing some basis for the frequent pairing of the adjectives "honest" and "hard-working." Less industrious individuals are not necessarily unproductive, but they do tend to be more laid back and more easily disinterested when the going gets tough.

You likely view yourself as a reliable worker and you take pains to avoid careless mistakes. Emotional Stability

An emotionally stable individual is not easily disturbed by daily hassles and recovers quickly when more substantial stressors inevitably occur. Emotional stability is not necessarily synonymous with consistent positivity, but simply implies lower levels of reactivity. Personality psychologists often refer to this factor as Neuroticism because that label has historically been used for the opposite end of the Emotional Stability dimension. The two aspects of Emotional Stability are Balance and Boldness (sometimes referred to as Volatility and Withdrawal, respectively). Balance reflects the ability to endure stress without getting flustered while Boldness represents the ability to face uncertain (or even threatening) circumstances with confidence. Individuals who are low in emotional stability, by contrast, are more likely to interpret everyday situations as threatening and more frequently experience negative emotions like worry, fear, anger, and sadness. As a result, individuals low on emotional stability are at greater risk for several types of mental illness.

Balance

Balance reflects the "stability" aspect of the Emotional Stability factor and some researchers prefer its opposite label, Volatility. Either way, the point is that balanced individuals are more even-natured. It's not that they never get agitated or upset - it's just that annoyances don't bother them as much. This also means that they tend to recover from setbacks more quickly. An individual who scores low on Balance may struggle with severe and prolonged negativity following an experience which has relatively little impact on an individual who scores highly on Balance. This aspect can have a strong effect on interpersonal relationships. Highly volatile individuals are often perceived as moody or unpredictable while extremely balanced individuals may be seen as impassive or aloof.

Many people find these emotional changes to be uncomfortable, though others sometimes claim that intense emotionality allows more volatile individuals to experience life more deeply. Individuals with very low scores on Balance are at elevated risk for mental illness.

Boldness

At its essence, the Boldness aspect of Emotional Stability relates to fear (or the absence of it). A more precise understanding incorporates the idea that fear and confidence are most salient in so-called "approach" situations. In other words, individuals who score low on Boldness often manage their apprehension by avoiding circumstances that they perceive as threatening or dangerous. Bold individuals tend to be more confident and may not even perceive danger in situations where most people are hesitant. While some degree of fearfulness is useful for protecting oneself from unnecessary danger, overly fearful individuals typically invest a great deal of time and energy worrying about unlikely outcomes.

Plasticity = Extraversion + Openness to New Experiences

Extraversion

Extraversion is perhaps the most widely recognized feature of human personality. The popularized notion of Extraversion is mainly limited to the idea that extraverts seek out social interaction while intraverts spend more time alone. Personality theorists suggest that the extraversion/introversion dimension is more accurately framed as a function of stimulation. Individuals who are more sensitive to outside stimuli (introverts) tend to prefer interacting in small groups. They also engage more often in independent, analytical, and cognitively-demanding activities. Extraverts are less sensitive to external stimuli and, as a result, seek more of it. They enjoy large gatherings and generally prefer gregarious conversation to thoughtful reflection. The two aspects of Extraversion are Assertiveness and Enthusiasm. While Assertiveness is negatively associated with Politeness and the Humility and Honesty aspects of Integrity, it is positively associated with Intellect and Openness. Enthusiasm is also negatively associated with Integrity and somewhat positively related to Compassion.

Enthusiasm

Individuals who are high on Enthusiasm behave exactly as the name suggests -- they are typically positive, energetic, warm and talkative. They enjoy being around other people and others typically enjoy their company. Individuals low on enthusiasm are often hard to get to know and they seldom engage in idle chatting. While very enthusiastic people are occasionally judged to be exhausting by those with less energy, most cultures place a high value on the Enthusiasm aspect of personality.

This may suggest that you don't feel it is important to engage in casual conversation or perhaps because you are somewhat uncomfortable around people who you don't know well. While you may enjoy events like large parties, you are probably most comfortable socializing in smaller groups or with a close friend.

Assertiveness

Assertive individuals are often described as having strong personalities. They tend to see themselves as effective leaders who are able to respond quickly to unexpected situation. They are rarely at a loss for words and tend to be energetic (though this is a more prominent feature of the Enthusiasm aspect). Individuals who are low on assertiveness are typically wary about attempting to lead or influence others without preparation. However, less assertive people tend to score more highly on Politeness, Honesty and Humility.

You may not be known as having a strong personality but you can still be effective at influencing others as long as you have time to gather your thoughts.

Openness to New Experiences

Openness to New Experiences (and Ideas) is probably the most broadly encompassing factor of personality. In fact, many people are surprised to learn that the two aspects (Intellect and Openness) are highly positively correlated. This positive association can be explained by the fact that both aspects share the common trait of being willing to engage with new ideas. Interestingly, both aspects of Openness are positively related to Extraversion and the Boldness aspect of Emotional Stability. This is probably driven by a generalized tendency towards "engagement." Individuals who are high on Openness will actively seek out answers and new experiences and these behaviors often overlap with Assertiveness and Boldness, though it should be noted that these tendencies may not involve the social component that is commonly associated with Extraversion. Openness also shares strong negative associations to both aspects of Integrity, though the basis for these relationships is not entirely clear. It seems that individuals who are high on Intellect and/or Openness to New Experiences are less likely to adhere to social norms regarding humble and honest behaviors.

Intellect

As an aspect of personality, Intellect represents the capacity and willingness to reason and engage with complexity. The extent to which Intellect can serve as a proxy for cognitive ability is not entirely clear, though most researchers acknowledge that there is considerable overlap. In other words, individuals who are high on Intellect are typically (but not always) intelligent, though they may be somewhat more or less intelligent than they are intellectual. And, of course, there are plenty of people with above-average intelligence who score low on Intellect because they either lack an accurate sense of their abilities or they do not enjoy engaging with complex ideas.

Openness

Openness is reflected in both components of the aspect's name. That is, it represents a combination of both open-mindedness and a desire for experiences. Prototypical examples often involve artistic appreciation -- highly open individuals are known to be avid consumers of visual art and music. While individuals who score low on Openness often enjoy the arts as well, they are less likely to report experiencing it on a deeply emotional level. Highly open individuals also claim to have vivid imaginations and spend more time engaging with abstract ideas. Individuals who score low on Openness, by contrast, are more likely to be viewed as normal and mainstream (as opposed to odd or strange).

Social Cohesion = Agreeableness + Integrity

Agreeableness

Compassion and Politeness are the two aspects of Agreeableness. While the two concepts may seem to be highly overlapping, they are no more highly correlated than the paired aspects in other factors. Empathy seems to be the feature which ties them both together. In other words, it seems that the ability to recognize others' emotions contributes to both compassionate and polite behaviors. Both of these aspects share positive correlations with the aspects of Integrity. Compassion is highly negatively correlated with Boldness while Politeness is negatively correlated with Assertiveness.

Compassion

Compassion is a highly valued virtue in most cultures and its meaning is reflected in its Latin roots -- "to suffer together." A key feature of compassion is the capacity for empathy or the ability to recognize the emotions being experienced by others. Compassion encompasses more than empathetic feelings however. Compassionate individuals take action to comfort those around them, especially those in need. The empathetic aspect of compassion is often demonstrated by frequent inquiries into the well-being of others and shared experiences of emotion. The active component typically involves a desire to give help and time to those who are suffering. Compassionate behaviors are often incompatible with behaviors of the Boldness aspect of Emotional Stability.

Your score on Compassion suggests that you are relatively indifferent to the feelings of others. This may reflect a lack of interest in other people's lives and/or a belief that others' should be more resourceful in dealing with their own problems.

Politeness

Politeness as an aspect of personality is somewhat different from the more common view of politeness as a measure of one's "manners." As part of Agreeableness, Politeness reflects an ability to tolerate the inevitable annoyances which result from interacting with others. This definition leads to some overlap between Politeness and the aspects of both Humility (part of Integrity) and Balance (part of Emotional Stability). Polite individuals typically report that they rarely feel anger or frustration with those around them. They tend to be patient and they rarely complain. By contrast, impolite people are easily annoyed and they may express their frustrations in a variety of ways -- they lose their tempers more frequently, hold grudges and are quick to make judgmental comments.

A low score on Politeness suggests that you are less tolerant of others' shortcomings than most people of your age and gender. The reasons for this may be reflected in your scores on other aspects. For example, a high score on Assertiveness may result in low Politeness due to an emphasis on action-oriented leadership whereas a low score on Humility may result in low Politeness due to a belief that being critical of others will make you look better by comparison. Or it may simply be that you're having an annoying day!

Integrity

Integrity is more commonly known by the names of its aspects (Honesty and Humility) and it is the only factor included in this report which is not one of the traditional "Big Five" factors of personality. Cross-cultural research suggests that the importance of this factor varies noticeably across countries. As might be predicted, both aspects of Integrity are positively related to Agreeableness. Less obvious associations include strong negative relationships with Boldness, Assertiveness, Enthusiasm, Intellect and Openness to New Experiences. Recent research has demonstrated that the Honesty and Humility aspects of Integrity are related to several other behaviors and traits including: faithfulness in romantic relationships, lower levels of creative ability, and lower levels of sensation-seeking (this is consistent with the negative associations to Extraversion).

Honesty

The defining feature of the Honesty aspect of Integrity is a strict adherence to behavioral "rules." This adherence typically extends beyond widely-held beliefs about stealing and cheating to include beliefs about how one should act in interpersonal relationships. Individuals who are less honest would be more likely, for example, to deceive others or cut corners when there are no perceived consequences. Highly honest individuals may also be more likely to feel badly when they fail to uphold these rules and social norms.

Your score was 4.
We estimate that you are in about the 27th percentile.

Your score on Honesty does not necessarily imply that you are a dishonest individual! To the contrary, it is sometimes the case that individuals with lower scores on this aspect are simply less troubled by relatively benign rule-breaking behavior. It's also ironically possible to get a low Honesty score by being more willing to disclose your dishonest behavior! Regardless of how this occurred, be sure to keep in mind that many types of dishonest behavior can get you into an awful lot of trouble.

Humility

The Humility aspect of Integrity is not only a reflection of arrogance (or its absence) but also relates negatively to the desire for power. Individuals who score highly on humility tend not to care about impressing others. By contrast, those who score low on Humility tend to place greater importance on status and power. As a result, they often invest considerable time and energy trying to impress those around them. While it is usually the case that social groups are intolerant of overt displays of arrogance and boasting, there can also be socially-reinforced reasons (and methods) for making oneself look as good as possible (and maybe even a little bit better).

Others may sometimes view you as boastful though this depends somewhat on the degree to which your high self-esteem is warranted. Cognition

While there are several competing theories in cognitive ability research, there is also growing consensus that abilities are best organized "hierarchically". In other words, each of the many different components of cognitive ability involve different sets of skills and these components are typically organized according to similarities among the tasks/skills involved. On the most narrow level, it is often difficult to distinguish between the skills used to accomplish a given task, but on the broadest level (at the top of the hierarchy), general skill sets are more plainly evident. For example, the skills required for different types of verbal tasks are more similar than those required for verbal and spatial tasks.

The items given here include several different types and we are actively working to develop more. In order to keep the test short, each participant is only administered a subset of the question types. Current question types include Alphanumeric Series, Matrix Reasoning, Cube Rotations, and Verbal-Educational (which includes general knowledge, logic and arithmetic questions).

You were given 16 cognition items and you responded to 16 of them. You answered 12 of these 16 items correctly. The average score on these same items among participants of your age and gender is 8.5. While scores on these items are positively correlated with commercial IQ measures, we discourage participants from considering their score to be a proxy for such measures. This "test" differs from most commercial tests in terms of brevity and the fact that it is administered online without a time limit in an un-proctored setting. It is important to take these factors into account when comparing your performance against the average. We recognize that many participants would like to know the correct responses for these cognitive ability items, but we do not currently make this information available in order to maintain the validity of the questions.
